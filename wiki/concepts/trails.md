# Trails

## Definition

Chains of associative connections through knowledge, originally conceived by Vannevar Bush for the Memex. A trail is a named sequence of linked items that captures a path of thinking or research, can be saved, shared, and extended.

## Key Principles

1. **Sequences of associations**: Not random links, but deliberate paths through related information
2. **Named and persistent**: Trails have identifiers and can be retrieved later
3. **Shareable**: Can be copied to other users' systems
4. **Extendable**: Can be merged with or branched from other trails
5. **Multiple membership**: Same item can appear in many different trails
6. **Capture thought process**: Trail represents the researcher's path of discovery

## How It Works

### Trail Creation (Memex concept)

1. User searches/retrieves first item
2. Creates association to related second item
3. Chains additional associations
4. Names the resulting trail (e.g., "bow-elasticity-crusades")
5. Trail stored for future retrieval
6. Can share trail by copying to another person's memex

### Trail Properties

**Bi-directional triggering**: Pulling up first item in trail automatically makes subsequent items available

**Branching**: User can branch off main trail to explore tangent, then return

**Merging**: Individual trails can join into broader, more general trails

**Layering**: Trails with pre-built trails (e.g., encyclopedia sold with trails already installed)

## Examples

### Bush's Turkish Bow Example

**Scenario**: Researcher investigating why Turkish bow outperformed English longbow in Crusades

**Trail built**:
```
[Encyclopedia: Turkish bow]
  → [Textbook: archery mechanics]
  → [Handbook: elasticity of materials]
  → [Historical: Crusades battles]
  → [Comparative: bow designs]
```

**Later use**: In conversation about innovation resistance, researcher retrieves trail, shows to friend

**Sharing**: Friend copies entire trail into their memex, merges it into broader trail about "human resistance to innovation"

### Modern Wiki Trail Example

### Implicit vs. Explicit Trails

**Memex (explicit)**: User manually builds and names trails

**Modern wikis (implicit)**: Backlinks and graph view show connections, but trails aren't explicitly captured as first-class objects

**LLM Wiki (hybrid)**: LLM creates cross-references (implicit trails), but valuable query sequences could be explicitly filed as trail pages

## Relationship to Modern Concepts

**Hypertext paths**: Following links through web pages = traversing implicit trail

**Wikipedia rabbit holes**: User's click path through related articles = ephemeral trail

**Wiki backlinks**: Show potential trails in reverse

**Obsidian graph view**: Visualizes all possible trails through knowledge graph

**Browser history**: Captures trails but not the meaningful associations

## Trail Blazers Profession

Bush envisioned "trail blazers" — people who:
- Find useful paths through knowledge
- Curate high-value trails
- Make their professional trails available
- Help others navigate complex domains

**Modern analogue**: 
- Wikipedia editors creating cross-references
- Curators of "awesome lists" on GitHub
- LLMs automatically discovering and maintaining trails

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Explicit vs. implicit**: Should trails be first-class objects (named, saved) or emergent from links? Bush said explicit; modern wikis mostly implicit.
- **Stale trails**: How to maintain trails as knowledge evolves? Add new items? Prune outdated ones?
- **Discovery**: How do users find relevant trails created by others? Search? Recommendations?
- **Granularity**: How long should a trail be? When to split or merge?
